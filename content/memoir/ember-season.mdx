## Part One: The Burning

There are moments that divide a life into before and after. For me, it was autumn—the season of embers, when everything seems to be burning away into ash. I remember the quality of light that day, how it fell through the windows at an angle that made everything look like it was on fire.

The details blur together now, which is perhaps a mercy. What remains is the feeling: a profound sense of wrongness, as if the ground beneath me had shifted and I was falling through space with no bottom in sight.

## Part Two: The Ash

The months that followed were characterized by numbness. I moved through my days like a ghost, present in body but absent in spirit. The world continued around me—people laughed, seasons changed, life went on—but I was suspended in a gray space where nothing felt real.

I learned that trauma doesn't announce itself with dramatic fanfare. It settles in quietly, like ash, coating everything with a fine layer of unreality. It changes how you perceive the world, how you relate to your own body, how you trust other people.

## Part Three: The Slow Burn

Recovery wasn't a moment; it was a process. It began with small things: the ability to feel the sun on my face without flinching, the capacity to laugh at something genuinely funny, the willingness to reach out to someone and say, "I'm struggling."

I discovered that healing isn't linear. There were days of progress followed by days of regression. There were moments of profound insight interrupted by flashbacks that felt like falling all over again. But slowly, imperceptibly, the balance began to shift.

## Part Four: Embers to Light

What I've learned through this journey is that trauma doesn't define us, but it shapes us. The scars remain, but they become part of our story rather than the whole story. The nervous system that once perceived threat everywhere can learn to recognize safety. The heart that felt shattered can learn to trust again.

I'm not the person I was before the burning. That person is gone, transformed in the fire. But I'm learning to be someone new—someone who has known darkness and found their way back to light. Someone who understands that resilience isn't about never falling; it's about the courage to get back up.

The ember season taught me that we are more resilient than we know. That healing is possible. That life can be rebuilt, not to what it was, but to something new and, in its own way, beautiful.

## Epilogue

I write this not as someone who has "recovered" in some final, complete sense. Recovery is ongoing. But I write it as someone who has learned to live with what happened, to integrate it into my story, and to move forward with hope.

If you're in your own ember season, know that the ash will eventually settle. The fire will burn itself out. And in the space left behind, something new can grow.
